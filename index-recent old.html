About

Most useful in the messy middle of building something

I sit between the people who want the thing, the engineers building it, and the users who decide whether it was worth it. That gap is where most projects quietly go wrong, and closing it is the part I'm good at. I've done it across health tech, ed-tech, SaaS, and e-commerce for the last ten years. Right now I'm a senior PM at Schoolfuel, building an AI platform that helps schools catch struggling students earlier.

On any given product I'm running discovery calls, writing the PRD, breaking it into Jira tickets, sitting in sprint planning, unblocking engineers, reviewing the build before it goes to QA, and then watching the numbers after launch to see whether we got it right. I've also owned the less glamorous but critical work: vendor contracts, payment integrations, and legal sign-off on a SaaS product before it ships.

What I care about is shipping things that hold up. Products that work, numbers that move in the right direction, and the guardrail metrics that quietly tell you whether you broke something else while chasing the headline figure. How I Work

One operator, three jobs that overlap

Product, project, and program management are different lenses on the same goal: get the right thing built and out the door. Here is what each looks like in my day to day. Tap through the three.

Deciding what to build, why, and how we will know it worked.
  • Discovery. I run user interviews (usually 60 or more per role), sit in on sales calls, send out surveys, and dig through product analytics in Mixpanel and Tableau to find where people actually drop off, not where we assume they do.
  • Definition. I turn what I learn into a PRD or BRD: the problem, the one success metric, the guardrail metrics, what is in scope, and what we are deliberately not doing this time.
  • Prioritization. I score the backlog with RICE, keep a triage queue for incoming bugs and requests, and protect the sprint goal so the roadmap does not get nibbled to death.
  • Tickets and backlog. I write Jira stories with clear acceptance criteria, attach the Figma screens so engineers are not guessing, and keep the backlog groomed a sprint or two ahead.
  • Launch and measure. I define the metric tree before launch, make sure the events are instrumented, pilot to a slice of users first, then watch the headline metric and its guardrails once it is live.
  • Iterate. The first version is a hypothesis. I read the data, talk to users again, and decide what to fix, double down on, or kill.
Getting a committed scope delivered on time, without surprises.
  • Cadence. Two-week sprints with planning on day one, a 15-minute daily standup, mid-sprint refinement, a demo at the end, and a retro to fix how we work, not just what we shipped.
  • Estimation. Planning poker with the team so commitments are realistic and owned by the people doing the work, instead of dates handed down from above.
  • Ceremonies and artifacts. Storyboard and design walkthroughs before build, a Definition of Ready and Definition of Done, and burndown tracking in Jira so we see slippage early.
  • Hand-offs and sign-offs. Design to engineering, engineering to QA, QA back to me for UAT sign-off, then release with the deploy engineer. Leadership signs off the PRD before build; marketing joins early and again at the QA stage to prep the launch.
  • Scope and risk. New mid-sprint requests go to the backlog unless they are a P0. When something has to come in, I make the trade explicit: if this goes in, that moves out. Change requests get logged with their impact, and a RAID log keeps risks from becoming surprises.
  • Quality gate. Nothing ships until it meets the acceptance criteria and clears UAT. I would rather move a date than ship something that fails its guardrails.
Running several launches at once so they add up instead of colliding.
  • Multiple workstreams. A typical program for me has three or four launches in flight at once, for example a payments revamp, a funnel redesign, and a reporting dashboard, each with its own squad but all pointed at one business goal.
  • Dependencies. I map where teams depend on each other (payments needing CRM events another squad owns is the classic one) and sequence the work so nobody sits blocked.
  • Release calendar. I stagger launches so QA and marketing are not drowning, and so two big changes never hit the same week and muddy each other's metrics.
  • Reporting. A weekly red, amber, green status to leadership with the numbers, the risks, and the specific decisions I need from them. No 20-slide updates.
  • Stakeholders. I keep finance, legal, ops, and outside vendors aligned so a launch does not stall on a contract clause or a compliance check at the last minute.
  • One scoreboard. Every workstream rolls up to the same top-line metric, so it is obvious which ones are pulling their weight and which need help.

Schoolfuel
Senior Product Manager, AI · Nov 2025 to Present

Schoolfuel is an AI student-success platform for schools. It does two things: it gives students an AI tutor that stays on their syllabus, and it gives teachers and counselors an early warning when a student is about to fall behind. I own the AI features end to end.

What I own
  • The roadmap for the AI tutor and the early-warning system, from discovery through launch.
  • The data and engineering partnership to build the models, and the FERPA and COPPA compliance that has to hold when you touch student data.
  • The success metrics and the guardrails that keep an AI product safe in a classroom.
How I work it
  • Discovery. Interviews with teachers, counselors, and students to find where students actually slip through the cracks, and what a teacher would trust enough to act on.
  • Definition. A tight first scope: an AI tutor grounded in the school's own material, and an at-risk flag that explains itself instead of spitting out a mystery score.
  • Build and pilot. Two-week sprints with data science and engineering, then a pilot in a small set of classrooms before widening, watching both the numbers and the teacher feedback.
The AI systems
  • AI tutor. Built on Microsoft Copilot and Azure OpenAI. I grounded it in each school's curriculum and the student's own progress so answers stay on-syllabus instead of wandering off into whatever the model feels like.
  • Early-warning model. A machine-learning model that scores risk from engagement, attendance, assignment, and grade signals, and surfaces the why, so a counselor sees the reasons behind a flag and can step in weeks earlier.
  • Personalized practice. Recommends the next lesson or practice set based on where a student is struggling.
  • How we keep it honest. I rate tutor answers on helpfulness and an on-syllabus check, run safety and hallucination filters, keep a human in the loop on any intervention, and check the risk model for bias across student groups.
Early results
+28%
Weekly active students in pilot classrooms, driven by the tutor giving fast, on-topic help.
~3 wks
Earlier risk detection. The early-warning model flags at-risk students roughly three weeks sooner than the old manual review.
40%
Less counselor review time because the model does the first pass and explains its reasoning.
Guardrails I watch: tutor hallucination and off-syllabus rate, false-positive rate on at-risk flags (so teachers do not lose trust), and fairness checks across student groups. Nothing auto-acts on a student without a human.
Built to FERPA and COPPA standards for student-data privacy.
STC Health
Senior Product Manager · Jan 2025 to Oct 2025

STC Health runs public-health software for state immunization and registry programs. The platform had grown into a tangle of legacy systems, so onboarding a new state was slow, finance had no clean view of cost per contract, and duplicate patient records kept creeping in. I owned the product side of pulling it into one compliance-ready cloud platform.

What I owned
  • The product roadmap for the modernization, plus the PRDs for onboarding, the finance and cost-center view, and patient data quality.
  • The vendor and supply-chain integration work, and the legal and compliance sign-off for a HIPAA and GAAP environment.
  • Reporting program status up to leadership and keeping engineering, data, finance, and implementation pointed the same way.
How I worked it
  • Discovery. I shadowed the implementation team through two real state onboardings, interviewed state admins and internal staff, and mapped every step from signed contract to go-live to find where the weeks were disappearing.
  • Definition. The biggest time sink was manual, state-by-state setup, so I wrote a PRD for a configurable onboarding workflow: reusable templates per state, bulk data import with validation, and a self-serve setup checklist.
  • Build. Two-week sprints with the squad. I broke the work into Jira epics, wrote stories with acceptance criteria, attached the Figma flows, and ran planning, standups, and retros. FHIR-based data models standardized the records the matching model worked from.
  • UAT and pilot. QA tested against the criteria, I signed off, and we piloted with two states before rolling wider, watching data-quality numbers the whole time.
  • Compliance. I worked with legal and finance so revenue recognition stayed GAAP-compliant and data handling stayed HIPAA-aligned, with sign-off built into the release gate rather than bolted on after.
The AI & data systems
  • ML record matching. The old deduplication ran on rigid rules, so I worked with the data science team to move patient matching to a machine-learning model (probabilistic entity resolution) that scores how likely two records are the same person, instead of a blunt pass or fail.
  • Tokenized patient search. We introduced a token system so staff could search and match patients across systems using secure, tokenized identifiers rather than raw personal data, which kept lookups fast and PHI protected.
  • Forecasting and anomaly detection. A demand-forecasting model for vaccine inventory, plus anomaly detection on incoming data to flag quality issues before they polluted the registry.
  • How we kept it honest. I measured the matching model on precision and recall, and routed any low-confidence match to a human for review, so the AI never silently merged two different patients.
What moved, and the lever
30%
Faster onboarding. Templated setup plus bulk import replaced manual, per-state data entry. I tracked onboarding cycle time in days from signed contract to go-live.
37%
Operational efficiency. One finance-and-supply-chain dashboard plus automated reconciliation replaced three hand-stitched reports, measured as throughput per person and manual hours saved.
42%
Fewer duplicate records. The ML matching model plus tokenized patient search cleaned the registry and made lookups reliable, tracked as duplicate rate per 10,000 records.
$1.8M+
Annual savings. Automation, vendor consolidation, and fewer manual errors, validated with the finance team against last year's run rate.
Guardrails I watched: data error rate and false-merge rate (so faster onboarding never came at the cost of merging two different patients), plus zero HIPAA audit findings on the new flow.
Operated under US GAAP and HIPAA for financial and regulatory integrity.
SeeMe.Ai
Co-founder & Product Lead · Jan 2023 to Dec 2024

SeeMe.Ai is an AI productivity app I co-founded for people who want to protect their focus and see where their day leaks. I led product from a blank page to a beta in the hands of real students.

What I owned
  • The whole product: the idea, the roadmap, the MVP scope, the analytics plan, and the pitch that brought in early funding.
  • Working with engineering on what to build first, and with finance mentors on the subscription model and burn rate.
How I worked it
  • Discovery. I ran more than 60 interviews with students and early-career professionals to understand how they actually plan a day and where they lose time. That killed a few features I was attached to, which is the point.
  • MVP first. Instead of a full planner, we shipped one core loop: set a goal, get AI nudges through the day, do a short evening review. I scoped it so we could learn fast rather than build everything.
  • Build and validate. Short cycles, a tight backlog, and a Mixpanel and Firebase setup from day one so we could see retention and which nudges actually got acted on.
  • Beta. We launched to 3,000+ ASU students, watched the cohorts, and tuned the nudge timing and frequency based on what kept people coming back.
  • Compliance. Since we touched student data, I built the workflows to FERPA and HIPAA standards before we scaled signups, not after.
The AI side
  • Built on Microsoft Copilot and Azure OpenAI. The assistant generated each user's nudges and suggestions, grounded in their own goals and recent activity so the advice was personal rather than generic.
  • An evaluation loop, not vibes. I tracked suggestion accuracy and the acted-on rate, and held a quality bar so we shipped nudges that were genuinely useful instead of just frequent.
  • The tradeoffs that matter for AI products. I watched nudge precision versus volume, kept an eye on hallucinated or off-base suggestions, and managed latency and cost per call as usage grew.
What moved, and the lever
+40%
Retention. Reworking the nudge timing and adding the short evening review gave people a reason to come back daily. I tracked D1, D7, and D30 retention and session frequency.
3,000+
Beta users. A focused campus rollout at ASU rather than a broad, shallow launch.
$500K
Pre-seed valuation. Early university funding secured off the back of the beta traction and the retention story.
Guardrails I watched: notification opt-out rate and nudge dismissal rate, so chasing retention did not just train people to mute us. If opt-outs rose, the nudge change got rolled back.
Great Learning
Product Manager, Product Ops & Revenue · Oct 2020 to Dec 2022

Great Learning sells high-ticket professional courses, so two things decide whether the business works: do learners finish and get a job, and can they actually pay without abandoning checkout. I owned product and revenue operations across both, including the payments and EMI experience.

What I owned
  • The learner funnel from first visit to enrollment to course completion and job outcome.
  • The payments and financing experience: more payment options, no-cost EMI for expensive courses, and clean reconciliation back to finance.
  • Revenue operations: the dashboards and forecast models that tied marketing spend to enrollment and retention.
The payments and EMI project (no code, all product)
  • The problem. A lot of learners dropped at the payment step. High course prices needed financing, but our options were thin and failed transactions were common.
  • Vendor selection. I ran a comparison of payment gateways and financing partners on payment success rate, settlement time, fees, API quality, and PCI-DSS compliance. I built the scorecard, negotiated fees with finance, and looped legal in on the data-processing terms.
  • Fitting it into our ecosystem. I worked with engineering to use a hosted, tokenized checkout so we never stored card data and stayed out of heavy PCI scope. We wired the payment events to our CRM and LMS so a successful payment auto-activated enrollment, and set up webhooks plus daily reconciliation files so finance could match every rupee.
  • UAT and pilot. I wrote test cases for success, failure, timeout, partial refund, and EMI approval and rejection, ran them in the gateway sandbox, then piloted on one course category before rolling out.
How I worked the funnel
  • Mapped lead to enrollment to job outcome for 5M+ learners, then attacked the biggest drop-offs with reminders, mentor allocation, and a clearer path to a credential.
  • Partnered with finance on monthly revenue dashboards and forecasts, and tightened coordination between pre-sales, marketing, and operations to cut churn.
What moved, and the lever
+33%
Course completion. Module-level reminders, mentor nudges, and removing dead ends in the path to a credential. Tracked as completion rate and drop-off by module.
$250K+
Yearly marketing savings. A better-converting funnel and an organic lead pipeline cut how much we leaned on paid acquisition. Tracked as CAC and lead-to-enroll conversion.
+22%
Operational throughput. Cleaner hand-offs between pre-sales, ops, and finance, plus the payments automation, removed manual steps per enrollment.
Guardrails I watched: payment success rate and refund and chargeback rate (so the new checkout did not trade volume for failures), reconciliation mismatch rate for finance, and assessment pass quality so completion did not rise by lowering the bar.

Revenue. New verticals plus better pricing and packaging, plus checkout that finally worked for non-metro learners. Tracked as category revenue and ARPU.
+71%
Course completion. Gamified retention and engagement features that kept cohorts active. Tracked as completion rate and session depth.
3x

Frameworks & Methodologies

The structure behind the intuition

I lean on a few frameworks so decisions are repeatable and not just gut feel. None of them are magic, they just keep a team honest about what matters and what to do next.

Agile and Scrum. Two-week sprints with planning, standups, refinement, demos, and retros. Stories carry clear acceptance criteria, and burndown tracking catches slippage early. This is how I cut delivery time by roughly 40% on a couple of teams.

Design thinking. Empathize, define, ideate, prototype, test. The interviews (600+ learners and 50+ healthcare users over the years) turn into journey maps, and the friction points on those maps turn into features.

Lean product. Ship an MVP to test the hypothesis before scaling it. Continuous experimentation and A/B testing decide what gets built next, which is how time-to-market dropped around 30%.

RICE for the main backlog, scoring reach, impact, confidence, and effort so the loudest request does not automatically win. It lifted roadmap delivery efficiency about 25%.

ICE for fast, early calls when we needed to test a feature quickly, which is how SeeMe.Ai got an MVP out in three weeks.

Eisenhower matrix to separate urgent from important when everything feels on fire, which cut cross-team escalations roughly 40%.

OKRs to align leadership, design, and engineering on a few measurable goals, like cutting onboarding time 30% or lifting data accuracy 42%.

KPI and guardrail tracking. Dashboards that link input to output to outcome, and a guardrail metric next to every headline one so we notice when a win in one place causes damage somewhere else.

North Star alignment. One metric per product that everything rolls up to, like retention for SeeMe.Ai or course completion for Great Learning.

Tickets and docs: Jira for stories, with Figma screens attached, and Confluence for notes, PRDs, and BRDs.
Design: Figma and Canva, depending on the team.
Analytics: Tableau and Mixpanel day to day, Power BI on earlier teams, and SQL at a working level for self-serve pulls.
Collaboration: Slack, Google Workspace, and Lucidchart.

Frameworks change from team to team. The habit underneath does not: align people, simplify the problem, and measure whether it actually worked.
